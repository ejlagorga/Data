Tufts Music 42 (Spring 2018):
History of Jazz

Eli LaGorga
4/29/2018


Duke Ellington “hated the spade work of writing music. (1)” When his orchestra's rehearsed, typically in the early hours of the morning after that nights performance had concluded, there were seldom scores. Duke sat at the piano and introduced themes he would like to develop, suggesting what each section should explore. This embedded freedom into the development process of his orchestra’s compositions. East St. Louis Toodle-Oo is so striking because the a dissonant assent by the trombones parried into a shrill trumpet solo collocated with the stereotypically bouncy swing era refrains could only have been adhered together after they had been developed individually. The result was a palpable panache unattainable by merely reserving some number of bars for a talented soloist to play freely in between pretentious corn-fed concert-hall syncopation.

This dynamism was crucial Ellington’s musical innovation but also to longevity of his big-bands. Talented soloists; bassist Jimmy Blanton, saxophonists Johnny Hodges and Harry Carney, and trumpeter Cootie Williams often stayed with him for extended periods. Together they achieved a level of familiarity and virtuosity that enabled Ellington to escape the conventions of band-section scoring and explore new harmonies which synthesised his musicians’ orthogonal sounds yet continued to emphasize congruent sections. For Ellington, a big-band wasn’t supposed to be three trumpets, three trombones, three saxophones, and a rhythm section with a guitar, piano, double bass, and drums all playing with such precision they sounded like a loud quartet; it was an assortment of individual voices, at a dinner party arguing. When his early contemporaries were concerned with creating a unified sound which flattened as many musicians as could be fit into a single voice, “Ellington believed in letting the dissonant voices of each musician play against each other. (1)”
